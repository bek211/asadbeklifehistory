About Me

My name is Asadbek Eshkobilov, a resilient, multi-skilled individual born in 2001 in rural Uzbekistan. My life story is not just a sequence of events, but a testament to hard work, adaptability, and relentless commitment to growth, even when opportunities were scarce.

My Complete Story

🏔️ My Family Background

I come from a large family living in a remote mountain village called Qoraobdol, in the Forish District of Jizzakh Region, Uzbekistan. There are seven of us in total — my parents, three sons, and two daughters. I am the eldest child, and with that comes responsibility.

We have never been a wealthy family — not by modern standards. Yes, we keep goats, cows, and chickens, and we have grapevines and fruit trees. But that isn't a sign of wealth in the village — it's a way of surviving and supporting ourselves. These animals are not luxury; they are part of our daily routine, part of the life we were born into.

We live far from the city. In fact, during drought years, we couldn't find water anywhere and had to dig our own well as a family. In our rural life, everything depends on physical labor. We don't have a big income or modern comforts. We don't own expensive technology, and we don't go on vacations. Instead, we work — every season, every day. We grow food, care for animals, and help each other.

Our life has been simple, even hard at times, but it taught me values that can't be bought — discipline, teamwork, humility, and gratitude. While we are not financially rich, we are rich in experience, effort, and family support. And that kind of wealth has shaped who I am today.

I completed my secondary education between 2008 and 2019, earning top marks in all subjects and numerous awards that recognized both my academic success and my contributions to school and community life. I was honored as "Best Student of the Year," achieved top places in district-level law Olympiads, and actively participated in team-based intellectual competitions at school. My schooling years were marked by a hunger for knowledge and a deep interest in history.

From 2012 to 2019, I was consistently an excellent student. My teachers truly knew my potential, and sometimes I wonder if I needed a school in the city with more opportunities to face real competition and reach my full academic potential.

This passion led me to study at the National University of Uzbekistan, one of the top 1000 universities globally, where I graduated in 2024 with a bachelor's degree in History. But learning for me has never been limited to the classroom. From a young age, I immersed myself in hands-on labor that shaped my character and broadened my skillset.

I come from a family of 7 people - my parents, 3 sons, and 2 daughters, with me being the eldest child. We are self-employed as family farmers and livestock keepers in our village. I worked extensively in agriculture: harvesting potatoes and fruits with care, tending to grapevines. For watermelon and melon work, I traveled to neighboring districts several times to help relatives during harvest seasons, as these crops don't grow in our village due to lack of rivers. I've spent many seasons working in the fields, adapting to extreme weather conditions while maintaining high productivity.

In addition, I worked in a textile workshop as a computerized embroidery machine operator, learning how to handle complex machinery and maintain high precision under pressure. I also worked in supermarkets, where I was responsible for warehouse operations, unloading goods, arranging products on store shelves, and keeping inventory organized.

In 2024, I joined Yandex as a courier, where I managed time-sensitive deliveries and developed better navigation and communication skills. These jobs weren't glamorous, but they gave me resilience and a deep respect for all forms of work.

2014-2025

🌾 Extensive Hands-On Experience Since Early Adolescence

Traditional Rural Life, Real Labor — Not a Hobby, a Way of Life

Growing up in a remote village about 100 km from the nearest city, I didn't spend my childhood visiting zoos or museums. My zoo was the livestock pen. My "museum" was a walnut tree — 12 meters high, and I had to climb it like an acrobat. 🎪 While other kids were feeding pigeons in the park, I was feeding sheep and goats — rain, snow, or blazing heat, it didn't matter. ☀️❄️🌧️ Responsibility doesn't take a holiday when you grow up in a family like mine.

From the age of 13 (maybe even earlier, but that's as far back as I clearly remember), I began helping my family with all kinds of tasks. This wasn't a one-man show — it was a family effort, but since I was the eldest child, I carried a heavier load. 💪 We weren't farmers in the commercial sense — we were traditional family caretakers, practicing age-old ways of animal husbandry and agriculture just to survive and stay rooted. 🌱

🐑 Animal Husbandry Experience — Family-Led, Survival-Driven

  • • Daily care of sheep, goats, and cows — regardless of season or weather 🌦️
  • • Grazing animals across wide fields in sun, wind, rain, and snow ❄️☀️
  • • Cleaning barns and sheds, collecting and preparing feed and hay 🏚️
  • • Shearing wool from sheep and goats during seasonal cycles ✂️
  • • Bathing animals in summer to prevent disease and ticks 🛁
  • • Climbing tall trees (10–12 meters high!) to harvest walnuts and fruit 🌳

🐐 Mixing herds: Goats and sheep grazed together — but don't be fooled, goats are a whole different story. They're energetic, mischievous, and curious about everything that moves (or doesn't). It's like herding comedians on four legs. 😄 We raised our animals not as part of a farm business but as a way of life, inherited from our ancestors. Goats especially required patience — they're hyper, noisy, and smart enough to test your nerves daily. 🤯 Sheep were calmer, but keeping them healthy through harsh Uzbek winters meant constant attention and tough skin. 🧤

🌾 Family Farming and Vineyard Work — The Hands That Feed Us

  • • Seasonal work in vineyards, fruit orchards, and fields 🍇🍎
  • • Picking apricots, apples, grapes, potatoes in our village 🥔🍇
  • • Traveled to neighboring districts to help relatives with watermelon and melon harvests 🍈🚛
  • • Climbing trees to pick fruit from places ladders feared to go 🪜
  • • Helping with manual irrigation, composting, and pest control 💧
  • • Hauling crops into trucks, sometimes 30–40 kg bags on my back 🚛

This wasn't a summer internship. This was daily life. And while some kids went to the zoo to look at animals, I was out there being responsible for them. Not watching from a distance, but up close and personal — shearing their wool, feeding them before I ate myself, and making sure they were safe and healthy. 🦆🐑

🏛️ Cultural Context

In Uzbek villages, this kind of life isn't called child labor — it's called growing up. 👦➡️👨 Boys learn hard work through doing, not theory. Our elders don't hand us life on a plate; they hand us a shovel, a rope, or a bale of hay and say, "Let's go." 🛠️ It's how we become men: through sweat, responsibility, and real-life experience.

And me? I wasn't just helping — I was learning. Building the kind of grit that no school can teach. 💎✨
